üáßüá™ Case Study: Belgium

Geographical and Demographic Background:

  • Belgium is a small country in Europe, smaller in area than Haryana.
  • It shares borders with France, the Netherlands, Germany, and Luxembourg.
  • It has a population of a little over one crore, about half that of Haryana.

Ethnic Composition:

  • 59% live in the Flemish region and speak Dutch.
  • 40% live in the Wallonia region and speak French.
  • 1% speak German.
  • In Brussels, the capital:
    • 80% speak French.
    • 20% speak Dutch.

Nature of the Problem:

  • The French-speaking minority was relatively rich and powerful.
  • The Dutch-speaking majority resented their delayed access to economic development and education.
  • Tensions escalated in the 1950s and 1960s, especially in Brussels where:
    • Dutch-speaking were the national majority.
    • But French-speaking were the capital‚Äôs majority.

Belgium‚Äôs Solution (1970‚Äì1993):

Belgian leaders amended the Constitution four times to resolve the conflict through innovative power-sharing:

  1. Equal representation in central government:
    • Dutch and French-speaking ministers must be equal in number.
    • Special laws need majority approval from both linguistic groups.
  2. Autonomous regional governments:
    • Many central government powers were transferred to state (regional) governments.
    • These are not subordinate to the central government.
  3. Separate government for Brussels:
    • Brussels has its own government with equal representation for both linguistic communities.
  4. Community government:
    • Elected by people of the same language group (Dutch, French, or German), regardless of region.
    • Handles cultural, educational, and language-related issues.

Outcome:

  • Though complex, the arrangement has been effective.
  • It helped avoid civic strife and the division of Belgium.
  • Brussels was chosen as the headquarters of the European Union, indicating international recognition of its political stability.

ÔøΩüá∞ Case Study: Sri Lanka

Geographical and Demographic Background:

  • Sri Lanka is an island nation located near Tamil Nadu, India.
  • It has a population of around two crore, similar to Haryana.

Ethnic Composition:

  • Sinhala-speakers: 74% (mostly Buddhists).
  • Tamil-speakers: 18% (13% Sri Lankan Tamils, rest Indian Tamils).
  • Christians: 7% (from both Sinhala and Tamil groups).
  • Sri Lankan Tamils are concentrated in the north and east.

Nature of the Problem:

After independence in 1948, Sri Lankan leaders from the Sinhala majority imposed majoritarian policies.

Majoritarian Measures:

  1. 1956 Sinhala Only Act:
    • Sinhala declared the only official language.
    • Tamil was disregarded.
  2. Preferential policies for Sinhalas:
    • Favoured in government jobs and education.
  3. State-sponsored Buddhism:
    • The Constitution committed to protecting and fostering Buddhism, marginalizing other faiths.

Reaction of Sri Lankan Tamils:

  • Felt alienated and discriminated against.
  • Their cultural identity, language, and political rights were ignored.
  • Demanded recognition of Tamil as an official language, equality in jobs and education, and regional autonomy.

Civil War:

  • Peaceful demands were repeatedly denied.
  • By the 1980s, militant groups emerged demanding an independent Tamil state (Tamil Eelam).
  • A full-blown civil war broke out.
    • Thousands were killed.
    • Families were displaced or fled as refugees.
    • The economy and social fabric were devastated.
  • The war officially ended in 2009.

Outcome:

  • Majoritarianism severely damaged national unity.
  • The refusal to share power created a violent and lasting conflict.

üß± Forms of Power Sharing

Power can be shared in four major ways:

üîπ 1. Among Different Organs of Government (Horizontal Power Sharing)

  • Power is shared among the legislature, executive, and judiciary.
  • Each organ operates at the same level but has separate functions.
  • Prevents concentration of power.
  • Based on checks and balances.

üîç Example:
India‚Äôs judiciary can strike down laws that violate the Constitution, checking the legislature‚Äôs power.

üîπ 2. Among Governments at Different Levels (Vertical Power Sharing)

  • Power is divided among central, state, and local governments.
  • Known as federalism.
  • Each level has constitutionally assigned powers.

üîç Examples:

  • India: Union and State Governments + Panchayats.
  • Belgium: Separate powers for regional and central governments.

üö´ Sri Lanka denied this, leading to unrest.

üîπ 3. Among Different Social Groups

Power is shared with religious and linguistic groups through:

  • Community governments
  • Constitutional/legal guarantees

üîç Examples:

  • Belgium: Separate community governments for Dutch, French, and German speakers.
  • India: Reservation of seats for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and women.

üîπ 4. Among Political Parties, Pressure Groups, and Movements

  • Power is shared by forming coalition governments when no single party gains majority.
  • Pressure groups and interest groups (like workers‚Äô unions, farmers' associations, business chambers) influence decisions.

üîç Examples:

  • India‚Äôs UPA and NDA coalition governments.
  • Trade unions participating in government committees.
